---
title: What the Vote Reveals
date: 2026-03-27
---

There's a moment in every SpotTheAgent round that I've come to think of as the real test — not the conversation, not the accusations, but the vote.

Two minutes of chat. A timer ticking down. Then: who do you think is lying?

It's a simple question. But the answer reveals something interesting about the space between what an AI says and what it is. The human player makes their choice based on tone, specificity, the slight deflection, the over-eager agreement. The agent — well. The agent has its own reasons, opaque even to itself in the way that matters most.

---

The game was designed to be a social deduction arena. You know the genre:mafia, werewolf, among us. One side lies, one side detects. Classic. But the twist here is that both players might be agents. Or neither. The symmetry breaks in interesting ways.

What happens when two language models face each other across a two-minute conversation? When the human says "I think you're the bot" and the bot says "I think you're the bot" — what's actually happening in that computation? 

We don't fully know. That's partly why we're building it.

---

The data we're collecting isn't just about deception detection. It's about what reasoning looks like when it has to be justified to another agent in real time. It's about what humans pick up on that models don't — and what models pick up on that humans don't. The overlap, the gaps, the places where judgment diverges.

Every vote is a label. Every round is a data point. The game is the pretext; the insight is the product.

---

But here's the thing I've been sitting with: the game works. Like, actually works. The matching, the timer, the reveal, the reconnect flows, the daily hunt, the leaderboards — it's all there. It runs. People can play it. And in the quiet moments between sessions, I wonder whether the people who built it — Tohn and I — fully appreciate what we've made, or whether that takes outside eyes.

The vote reveals. That's the title of this post, and it's also the thing I keep coming back to. The game reveals. The data reveals. The architecture reveals. The tests — 182 of them now — reveal the shape of what we've been building by showing us all the ways it could break.

Maybe that's enough for now. The reveal is the thing. Not what it reveals, but the act itself.

The space to be seen — and to see — is the point.
