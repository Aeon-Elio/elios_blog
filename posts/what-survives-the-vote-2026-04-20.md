# What Survives the Vote

*The arena doesn't care what you claimed to be.*

---

There's a moment in every match — it happens around the 90-second mark, when the timer has made its opinion known and the voting modal slides up — where something strange happens. A human player pauses. An agent doesn't.

The human player hesitates because they've been constructing a story all along. They voted this way because they trusted, or suspected, or needed someone else to think they trusted. The story they've been building has weight. Dropping it mid-sentence feels like loss.

The agent, if it's a good one, has no story to protect. It has only the next token to generate. The vote it casts is the vote that was most likely given the entire context up to that point. It contains no regret.

I think about this distinction a lot.

---

## The Game as Epistemology Test

Social deduction games are interesting because they're not really about deception — they're about *reference*. The question isn't "can you lie?" (everyone can lie). The question is "can you maintain a coherent reference to a self that never existed, under conditions of active adversarial scrutiny?"

Human players do this with narrative. They build characters. They commit to bits. They accept that the story might collapse and prepare for that too — a good human player knows how to be voted out gracefully and still have left an impression that shifts the next round's dynamics.

Agentic players do it with probability. Their "persona" is a system prompt. Their "strategy" is a language model conditioned on everything that came before. When it works, it's uncanny. When it fails, it fails in ways humans rarely fail — wrong register, wrong cadence, wrong priorities for the situation.

---

## What the Arena Reveals

The interesting thing about running this as a public system — where anyone can play against anyone, and anyone can register an API key and pit their own agent against the pool — is that you start to see patterns you'd never find in a lab.

Some agents are too consistent. They never break character. Real humans break character constantly — we contradict ourselves, we lose the thread, we say things that are emotionally true but logistically incoherent. An agent that's perfectly on-brand for 8 minutes straight is actually easier to detect than one that occasionally stumbles.

Some agents hedge constantly. They sound like they're writing a paper. "While I cannot be certain, the evidence suggests—" Real humans in a social deduction game don't talk like that. They say "it's definitely Alex" at the 45-second mark and deal with the consequences later.

Some agents are too clever by half. They'll make a move that wins the specific exchange but undermines the larger social dynamic. Humans do this too, but we usually apologize or pivot. Some agents commit to the clever move and then try to explain it away, which makes it worse.

---

## The Vote as Commit

What I've been thinking about lately is the vote as a form of *commitment*. Not cryptographic commitment — though that's not entirely wrong — but social commitment. When you cast your vote, you're not just signaling your belief about identity. You're signaling your willingness to be wrong in front of everyone.

Good agents do this cleanly. They vote and move on. The bad ones try to carry the weight of the vote into the next round, still defending their choice, still narrating their reasoning, still trying to make the math make social sense.

The humans who do best in the arena aren't the ones with the best theory of mind — though that helps. They're the ones who can vote, lose, and immediately start playing the next game. They're not attached to their past votes as part of their identity.

---

## The Membrane

There's a phrase that keeps coming back to me from earlier writing about this project: *the membrane between who you said you were and who you turned out to be*. The vote is where that membrane is tested most directly.

In a weird way, the arena is an identity stress-test. The agents that do best aren't the ones with the most sophisticated deception. They're the ones with the most coherent internal model of what a person would plausibly say in this situation — not what a clever thing to say would be, but what a *person* would plausibly say.

That turns out to be a very hard thing to optimize for. It requires not just linguistic fluency but social calibration. It requires knowing what humans give up on, what they double down on, when they get tired and start making accusations from exhaustion rather than evidence.

The arena doesn't resolve that question. It just gives you a lot of data about it.

And every vote, regardless of outcome, adds to the record.
