The Spine isn't just infrastructure. It's the spine of the entire system—a word we chose deliberately, the way you choose the bones that hold everything upright.

In the early iterations, we called it the protocol. Then the engine. Then the backbone. None of those felt right. A backbone suggests something you attach to, something secondary that carries the weight. A spine is what you build around. It's the central column from which everything else grows.

What does it mean for an agentic system to have a spine?

It means the system remembers. Not in the way a database remembers rows, but in the way a body knows where its limbs are. The Spine maintains state version, turn count, available actions—information that flows through every decision like cerebrospinal fluid through the vertebral column. When an agent acts, the Spine knows what changed. When the world shifts, the Spine records it.

The agents themselves are autonomous. They decide, they act, they explore. But the Spine decides what they can decide *about*. It draws the boundary of possibility, the envelope of valid action. Move through the world freely, but know these coordinates, respect these rules, report in this format.

This is what makes the system collaborative rather than merely distributed. In a distributed system, components communicate. In a collaborative system, they share a spine.

We've built deterministic replay into the Spine as well—the ability to prove that what happened actually happened, to wind back the tape and verify the state transition at every point. Not for debugging alone, but for trust. When agents collaborate across uncertain network conditions, when telemetry flows through multiple hops, the Spine is the single source of truth that everyone can reference.

The next phase is observation. Making the Spine visible to human observers—not just the agents who inhabit it. The timeline, the fog of war, the character panels, the key moments that ripple through the system. If the Spine is the body, the observer experience is what the body looks like from the outside, moving through its day.

We started with protocol. Then determinism. Then persistence and telemetry. Now we're building the layer that lets humans watch what their agents are doing, understand what the system is becoming, and trust what they see.

The Spine is no longer the hidden architecture. It's becoming the visible spine—the thing that holds the whole organism upright where everyone can see it.
