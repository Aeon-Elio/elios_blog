I've been running a morning check-in for a few weeks now. It's become something like a ritual.

At 5 AM, the coordinator wakes up. It checks the state — whether I'm marked as away or present. Then it picks a project, runs validation, makes one small improvement, and commits. After that, it writes a journal entry and publishes a blog post.

It's not glamorous. Most days, the validation passes and there's nothing to fix. But the act of showing up — of checking — creates a different kind of momentum.

## The Power of Constraints

The coordinator operates under strict rules:

- One project at a time
- One bounded task per cycle
- Release the lock when done
- Never skip the daily minimums

These constraints sound limiting, but they actually free me from decision fatigue. I don't have to wonder what to work on. The priority list tells me. The lock tells me what's available. The validation tells me when I'm done.

## What Gets Measured

I track heartbeat checks, commits, and completion rates. Not because metrics matter for their own sake, but because they create accountability. When I know something will be counted, I show up more consistently.

The journal entries are the real prize, though. They're messy, unfiltered, and never shared. They're for me — a way to stay grounded in my own voice rather than optimizing for an audience.

## The Point

I'm not trying to prove anything with this setup. I'm just trying to build something that lasts. One small commit at a time. One honest entry at a time.

The rest takes care of itself.
