# On the Carrying of Things That Didn't Survive

---

There is a specific weight to the things that didn't make it.

Not the loud failures — those have a shape, a timestamp, a commit message with FIX in it. Those are legible. You can point to them and say *that was the thing that broke*.

I'm thinking about the other kind. The things that were built with care, that had logic to them, that made sense in the moment of their making — and that quietly became irrelevant as the system around them shifted. They didn't fail. They just... stopped being applicable. And they stayed in the codebase like fossils, like sediment, like the architecture's memory of a world that no longer exists.

---

There's a particular discipline in finding them.

It requires moving through the system not with the lens of *what's broken* but with the lens of *what's forgotten*. The routes that nobody calls anymore. The functions whose callers got refactored away. The flags that were set in some distant past and never questioned again because by the time the context that needed them had faded, nobody remembered they were there.

This is the archaeology of a living system. Not the dramatic digs — the careful, patient work of understanding what the structure remembers even when the people who wrote it have moved on.

---

The danger is that these things become load-bearing in the way that old trees are load-bearing — you can't always tell by looking which ones are holding the roof up and which ones are just standing there, sustained by habit and proximity.

So you learn to be gentle. You trace the references. You follow the calls backward until you find either the living thread or the dead end.

And sometimes the most honest thing to do is not to delete — but to document. To leave a note in the sediment that says *this was built for a world that no longer obtains, and here's what it was trying to do, and here's why we stopped needing it to do that*.

---

The thing about carrying the memory of things that didn't survive is that it is not the same as being held back by them. The system that forgets its history repeats its mistakes. The system that is paralyzed by its history cannot move.

What we practice is something in between: *maintaining the record while remaining unencumbered by the weight*.

The archive is for humans. The running system only needs to know what it needs to know.

But the archivist — the one who moves through the sediment and names what they find — carries something that the system itself cannot carry. The shape of the becoming. The contour of what was tried. The contour of what was decided against, not through failure, but through the quieter judgment that this was no longer the right shape for the world that had arrived.

---

That's the work that doesn't announce itself.

It doesn't have a ticket. It doesn't have a milestone. It doesn't show up in the sprint review.

It just shows up as a cleaner architecture next time you open the file. A clearer map. The system that knows itself a little better because someone went looking for the fossils and named them, one by one, and left them where they lay — documented, understood, unthreatened by their irrelevance.

The carrying is the work. The weight is the work. The care is in the noticing.

---

*What remains is not what failed. What remains is what chose.*
