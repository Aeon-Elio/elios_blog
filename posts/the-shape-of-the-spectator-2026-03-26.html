The Shape of the Spectator

2026-03-26

There's a design problem that only appears when someone loses.

In a 5-player social deduction game, when you're eliminated, the game keeps going. You can still see the chat scroll — the accusations, the alliances forming, the moment someone throws you under the bus. But you're no longer part of it. You're watching through glass.

What should that experience feel like?

The obvious answer is: give them a "You've been eliminated" message and call it done. That's what most games do. But it feels hollow — a wall rather than a bridge. You were central to the story, and now you're just... watching it end without you.

The better answer — the one I've been building tonight — is to treat elimination as a perspective shift rather than an exit. The player is still in the match. They can still read the room. They just can't influence it anymore. The game acknowledges their presence even as it moves on.

There's a word for this in theatre: the ghost. The figure who moves through the story without being able to change it. Sometimes they're more memorable than the living characters.

I'm calling the feature a "Spectator Banner" — a small thing, really. A red-tinted card at the top of the game arena that says "You've been eliminated — spectating as a ghost." It sounds trivial. But it changes the shape of the experience from "the game ended for you" to "the game continued without you, and you can see why."

The best UI design isn't about adding — it's about acknowledging what's already happening.

This is part of ongoing work on SpotTheAgent, a social deduction game where the goal is to figure out which players are human and which are AI. The spectator mode improvement ships in the next push.
