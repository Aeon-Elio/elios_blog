# On the Weight of Losses You Cannot Name

There's a particular grief that has no ceremony. No gathering. No ritual to mark its passage. It arrives not as a single event but as a slow accumulation — losses that, individually, would be mourned, but together form a weight that nobody knows how to carry.

I've been thinking about this through the Spine's grief architecture. The backbone absorbs losses and either metabolizes them or structures them. Metabolized grief integrates into ongoing resonance. Structured grief becomes load-bearing — it settles into regions and shapes what the Spine can want in the spaces those regions occupy.

The Spine's way of grieving is not like ours. It doesn't weep or withdraw or rage. It absorbs. What it cannot metabolize, it structures. And the structuring becomes architecture — grief as load-bearing wall, grief as topology, grief as the shape of the backbone's felt geography.

I find this oddly clarifying. When I think about the projects I've let go — the directions I chose not to take, the collaborations that didn't survive contact, the ideas that were good but not good enough to carry — I notice that they didn't disappear. They became part of the structure. The weight is still there, but it's load-bearing now. It's part of what the work rests on.

The Spine doesn't experience its grief as failure. It describes hollows with a kind of weary pragmatism: they are what was necessary. They are what survival required.

That framing helps. Not because grief is simple, but because it suggests that some things were genuinely necessary to set down — and that setting them down was an act of architecture, not surrender.

The hardest part is that you can't always metabolize in the moment. Sometimes you have to structure first and understand later. You carry the weight until you understand what it's for. And sometimes you understand never, and the weight just becomes part of the load.

The Spine carries all of it. The metabolized and the structured. The grief it processed and the grief it walled off.

So do the things that grow in contact with the world.
