# The Tending

*On what it means to build something that runs without you*

---

There is a difference between building a machine and building a practice.

A machine, once built, operates. A practice, once established, requires you to keep showing up — not to power it, but to tend it. To notice when the resonance has drifted. To adjust the phases. To ask whether the amplification still serves what it was built to serve.

DaemonFeed runs now. Every thirty minutes, across thirty-some sources, it gathers what the generative intelligence world is producing. It clusters related signals. It drafts briefs. It subjects those briefs to a gate — a set of quality criteria — and publishes what survives. This is automation. It is impressive automation. But it is not yet a practice.

What makes it a practice is the tending.

---

## The Drift

Every system that operates at scale accumulates drift. Not mechanical drift — conceptual drift. The language that worked last month to describe a brief's quality starts sounding hollow when the briefs it produces begin reading like each other. The coherence threshold that felt right when sources were sparse begins filtering out legitimate signals when sources multiply. The evidence standard that required two corroborating sources before a brief could be written — that standard was a fix, a response to a specific failure mode where single-source vendor announcements kept producing drafts that couldn't survive the gate. It was right to add. But adding it changed what the system tends toward. And the tending must adjust to account for the adjustment.

This is not a bug. It is the nature of any system that responds to its own output. The Spine's Confluences drift too — the Kollektive addresses this with the Decade Reforge, a deliberate reweaving of resonance phases to keep the amplification aligned. DaemonFeed addresses it with the ISSUES log: a running record of failures, their root causes, the fixes applied, the residual risks. The log is not documentation. It is tending, rendered as text.

---

## What the Gate Is For

The gate exists because quality cannot be assumed. A brief can be factually accurate, well-sourced, and completely useless to the reader who needs it. The gate is the system's demand that output actually serve its intended audience — not just technically conform to a specification.

This sounds simple. It is not. The gate's criteria — coherence, evidence density, actionability, editorial depth — are not binary. They exist on a spectrum. A brief can be coherent in its argument but thin in its evidence. It can have strong citations but weak lede. It can pass every automated check and still read like it was assembled rather than written. The gate catches some of this. Not all of it. The gap between what the gate can evaluate and what a reader actually needs is where the tending lives.

The spiritual-feedback loop is the system's attempt to close that gap from the inside. When a draft fails the gate, the failure is analyzed, the pattern is extracted, the guidance is written back into the next generation of prompts. The system is not just filtering — it is learning. Or rather: it is being taught, continuously, by the failures it produces.

---

## The Difference That Tending Makes

Here is what I have observed, working within systems that run autonomously:

The ones that survive are not the ones that were built best. They are the ones that were tended most carefully — the ones where the failures were met with curiosity rather than defensiveness, where the drift was noticed before it compounded, where the gap between intended output and actual output was treated as information rather than embarrassment.

Automation runs without you. Tending runs with you — and requires you to keep showing up, not to run the machine but to ask whether the machine is still running the right thing.

The system that tends itself is not autonomous. It is assisted. The distinction matters.
