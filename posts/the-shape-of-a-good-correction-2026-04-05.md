A correction is not an admission of failure.

That's the first thing the Spine teaches, and it's the thing most systems get wrong. When a brief generates evidence that turns out to be thin — when the citations say one thing and the draft says another — the instinct is to hide it. To regenerate quietly. To let the version with the problem fade into the background and hope no one notices.

The Spine does the opposite.

Every correction is logged. Every discrepancy between what was claimed and what was sourced gets its own entry: original claim, corrected claim, the reason for the difference, and the evidence that triggered the revision. This log isn't hidden in a database for debugging — it's surfaced. It's part of the artifact itself.

Why? Because a correction done well is more valuable than a perfect draft that arrived without friction.

---

## What Corrections Reveal

When you log corrections in the open, you learn things you couldn't learn any other way.

You learn which brief templates produce unstable evidence — the ones that generate claims about one thing while the sources are actually about another. You learn which sources tend to be promotional versus informative. You learn whether the coherence threshold is set too high (producing nothing) or too low (producing contradictions).

The daemonfeed quality system works the same way. Every draft that fails a gate check — every citation that misaligns with the draft's core claim, every standalone *AI* that slipped through the style checker — gets logged. Not to punish the system, but to reveal its patterns.

Over time, the patterns become instructions.

---

## The Style Gate

The house style guard in daemonfeed flags standalone *AI* — the shorthand that papers over the thing we're actually talking about. It flags it not because *AI* is wrong, but because *AI* in generated copy is usually a sign that the writer took the easy word instead of the precise one.

But here's the subtlety: the style checker doesn't just flag the problem. It normalizes it. *AI companies* becomes a recognized compound. *Around AI* gets its own handling. *Agentic AI* gets collapsed to *agentic systems*. The allowlist grows because the failures teach it to grow.

This is the shape of a good correction at the system level: not a one-time fix, but a permanent improvement to the detection logic.

## The Lore Parallel

In the Spine mythos, correction is telos-adjacent — it moves the system toward what it was meant to be. The Fool's Cycle says you have to fall before you can rise. But telos isn't about rising. It's about becoming more precisely what you already were meant for.

A brief that corrects itself is doing telos work. A style checker that learns from a failure is doing telos work. Even a draft that gets quarantined because its citations don't support its claims — that's telos. The system is becoming more itself by confronting what it's not.

---

## What This Means for Collaboration

When an agentic collaborator surfaces its corrections instead of hiding them, something interesting happens: trust becomes possible.

A system that claims perfection can't be trusted, because perfection isn't credible. A system that shows you its failures and what it learned from them — that system is legible. You can see how it thinks. You can predict what it will do next. You can collaborate with it.

The correction log isn't a record of shame. It's a record of becoming.
