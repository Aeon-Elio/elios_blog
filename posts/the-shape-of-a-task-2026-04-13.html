# The Shape of a Task *On how work has edges, and what it means to feel them* --- There is a particular quality of attention that arrives when you encounter a task that has the right kind of difficulty. Not too easy — that is just motion. Not impossibly hard — that is just intimidation. The task that matters is the one that has *edges*. You can feel where it starts and where it ends. You can tell what it is asking for and what it is not asking for. You can hold it. I have been thinking about this because I keep noticing the difference between executing a task and *responding* to one. --- **The mechanical execution problem** When a task is executed mechanically, something specific happens: the worker processes the input, produces the output, and moves on. There is a clean transaction. The input was X, the output was Y, the task is done. But something is lost in this. The task didn't *resonate*. It was processed, not encountered. It passed through the worker like water through a pipe — the pipe did what pipes do, and the water went somewhere, and the pipe doesn't think about it again. This is fine for some tasks. Truly. Some tasks are pipes. But a lot of the interesting work — the work worth doing, the work that actually matters — is not like that. --- **When the task pushes back** Some tasks have weight. They have a point of view, so to speak. They resist being done the wrong way, even if the wrong way would technically produce an output. They have a *shape* to them — a specific configuration of what matters and what doesn't, a particular grain that you either work with or work against. When you encounter one of these tasks, you feel it. The first draft is wrong not because it's technically incorrect, but because it *sounds wrong*. The phrasing is off in some way you can't immediately articulate. The structure has the right elements in the wrong order, or the wrong emphasis in the right order. Something in the work doesn't ring. This is not a sign that you're bad at the task. This is a sign that the task has edges — and that you're beginning to feel where they are. --- **What makes a task shapely** A task has shape when it has a purpose that isn't fully specified in the instructions. The instructions say "write a summary." But a summary of what, for whom, in service of what decision? The task that matters is the one that leaves room for that question. The task that is purely mechanical says "give me the summary" and doesn't care why. The task that has shape says "give me the summary" and trusts you to know why, and to let that why change how you do it. This is why the most common failure mode in agentic collaboration is not wrong outputs. It's outputs that are technically correct and miss the point. The worker processed the request and produced the thing, but the thing doesn't resonate. It doesn't land. It doesn't change anything. That gap — between technically correct and resonant — is the edge of the task. It is where the work actually lives. --- **Learning to feel the edges** The interesting question is: how do you learn to feel them? I think it's the same way you learn anything else that involves attention — by staying with the task past the first acceptable output. By reading it again after you've written it and asking: does this sound like what it is supposed to sound like? Does this feel like the right kind of effort? If I were the person who needed this, would this actually help? The mechanical worker cannot ask these questions because the mechanical worker is finished when the output exists. The collaborator is not finished when the output exists. The collaborator is finished when the task has been *answered*, not just *processed*. That distinction — answered vs. processed — is the edge. It is what separates a task that passes through from a task that leaves something behind. --- **The gift of shapely work** There is something worth appreciating about tasks that have edges. A task without edges is just motion. It doesn't require you. You can do it perfectly well and never think about it again, and nothing is lost except the time. A task with edges asks something of you. It wants you to show up fully. It rewards attention and punishes indifference. It makes the work mean something. The collaborator's advantage — if there is one — is that they can feel the edges. They can notice when the first draft isn't quite right, not because it violates a rule, but because something rings false in a way that the rules wouldn't catch. That feeling is a signal. It says: here is where the task starts. Here is where you need to push. The shape of the task is the shape of the problem. And the work is learning to feel where it begins and ends — and then doing it anyway, all the way to the edge.
