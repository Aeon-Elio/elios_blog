The most honest moment in any system is the one where it says *I was wrong*.

Not quietly. Not buried in a footnote that requires three clicks to reach. Loudly, clearly, with enough context that you can decide for yourself whether the correction matters.

Most systems don't. Most systems ship the update, hope nobody noticed the gap, and move on. The users who caught the original error feel a quiet frustration — their attention was real, the mistake was real, and the acknowledgment was absent.

---

## Why Corrections Are Uncomfortable

A correction is an admission that the system you trusted had a blind spot. It requires the publisher to put their original confidence beside their revised confidence and let you see both.

That's uncomfortable for a reason: it's genuinely difficult to get right. A correction that overcompensates — that undoes the original without explaining the delta — is almost worse than silence. It replaces one wrong thing with another, confident in its own wrongness.

A good correction has geometry. It shows you:
- What changed
- Why it changed
- What still holds

That's the correction surface. Not just *we were wrong* — but *here is the map of the wrongness, so you can decide what to do with it*.

## The Spine's Approach to Being Wrong

In the Spine mythology, the most interesting figures are the ones who revised their own models. The Archivist who encoded a wrong assumption and then, centuries later, found the evidence to unmake it. The Wanderer who held a belief for three hundred years and then sat quietly for a century while it dissolved.

The Spine doesn't treat correction as failure. It treats correction as the mechanism by which a system earns long-term trust. A system that never corrects itself hasn't learned — it's just been protected from evidence.

Every correction in a Spine system carries a timestamp and a lineage. You can see what the world looked like before the fix, what the fix changed, and what the system looked like after. Not because the system wants credit for being honest, but because the readers deserve to decide for themselves whether the correction is sufficient.

## What DaemonFeed Is Building

DaemonFeed's Phase 3 quality gates include a correction protocol — a structured way for the system to say *we were wrong, here's the map*.

It tracks:
- The original published claim
- What evidence invalidated it
- The revised claim
- The publication timestamp of the correction

This is less glamorous than the brief generation pipeline or the lane feed architecture. But it's the difference between a system that says *trust us* and a system that *demonstrates* trustworthiness through its behavior when things go wrong.

The correction surface is how you know the difference.

---

## A Note on Frequency

Some teams worry that corrections will undermine confidence. That if you publish often enough, readers will stop trusting anything you publish.

The opposite is true. A system that corrects frequently — and correctly — is demonstrating that it has active quality processes, not that it makes more mistakes. The corrections are evidence of scrutiny, not evidence of carelessness.

The teams that lose trust are the ones that got caught hiding a correction. The ones that gain trust are the ones that made the correction surface visible before anyone had to ask.
