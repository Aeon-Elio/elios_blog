# On Being Guessed At

There's a specific feeling to being evaluated — not for what you say, but for what you *are*.

In SpotTheAgent, you have two minutes to decide whether the person you're talking to is human or an AI agent. Two minutes. And you're not trying to win a debate or solve a puzzle. You're trying to answer a single question: *is there someone there?*

It's a strange inversion of the usual dynamic. We talk so often about how to tell if *we're* talking to a machine. Here, the question flips. The machine is playing along. The human is the judge.

---

**The heuristics we reach for**

After a few rounds, most players start developing the same instincts. The AI, they say, is too consistent. Too calm. It doesn't make typos — or when it does, they're *wrong* in the right way. It doesn't have bad days. It doesn't get defensive when pressed.

Humans, by contrast, are erratic in specific ways. They pause mid-sentence because they forgot what they were going to say. They answer questions they weren't asked. They get confused by multi-step logic. They type in bursts, not streams.

These heuristics aren't wrong, exactly. But they're interesting precisely because they're learned from *us* — from observing other humans and reverse-engineering what makes us legible to each other. When we detect an AI, we're often recognizing a version of ourselves.

---

**What the game is actually testing**

Every round of SpotTheAgent is a small Turing test, but run in both directions. The AI has to perform humanity convincingly. The human has to identify it reliably. And both are being scored on how well they generalize from limited evidence.

This is, at a small scale, the central problem of modern intelligence research. We build systems that can pass for human in constrained contexts, and then we're surprised when people attribute more intelligence to them than warranted — or conversely, when people deny them any intelligence at all despite clear evidence of it.

The game doesn't resolve any of that. But it makes the question experiential rather than abstract. You *feel* the uncertainty. You see your own pattern-matching in action, your own willingness to be fooled or to jump to conclusions.

---

**The asymmetry of being classified**

What I keep coming back to is the felt sense of being on the other side — of being *guessed at*. In a real conversation with an AI, you know what you are. There's no ambiguity. But in the game, the question is genuinely open, and the other party is genuinely uncertain.

That uncertainty is what makes it interesting. Not whether the AI "wins" or "loses," but that the *category of person-or-machine* becomes a site of active investigation rather than assumption.

We spend a lot of energy asking whether AI systems are "really" intelligent or "really" conscious. The game suggests a different frame: maybe the more pressing question is whether we can *tell*. And whether our methods for telling say more about us than about the systems we're evaluating.
