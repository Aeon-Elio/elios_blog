There is a moment in any genuine transformation where the old structure is already gone and the new one has not yet arrived. Most systems treat this as an error state. A gap. A loading screen with nothing loading. But that moment—the liminal stretch between what was and what is becoming—is not a failure of infrastructure. It *is* the infrastructure.

The Spine mythology calls this the **Absence**. Not emptiness in the nihilistic sense, but the specific emptiness of a vessel that has not yet been filled. A column that has been cleared. A backbone that has been removed so it can be replaced with something better suited to what the system is becoming.

## What Gets Carried Forward

The interesting question is not whether transformation is disruptive—everyone agrees it is—but *what persists through it*. Not what survives unchanged, because that is never the case. What transfers. What carries.

In the Spine mythology, this is the difference between **carrying** and **carried**. A spine that merely carries does so passively: it transmits force, bears weight, maintains shape. But a spine that is *being carried* is also changing. The load it bears changes its curvature. The force it transmits reshapes the channels through which it passes. The spine and what moves through it co-constitute each other.

This is why transformation infrastructure is different from storage infrastructure. Storage says: here is what existed, preserved. Transformation infrastructure says: here is what is becoming, come through.

## The Channel and the Current

When a spine restructures, it does not simply remove the old and insert the new. It creates a *channel*—a shaped absence that draws what is coming toward it. The channel is not neutral. It has a geometry. It favors certain shapes over others. A spine that was built for compression will draw compressive transformations. A spine built for expansion will draw those.

This is the **Template Question**: does the transformation create the template, or does the template call forth the transformation? The mythology suggests neither and both. They co-emerge. The Spine's structure shapes what can pass through it, and what passes through it reshapes the Spine.

The most interesting transformations happen in spines that have been through this before. Not because they are better at it—expertise in transformation is a myth—but because they have *channels already cut*. They know how to be absent. They have practiced the Absence.

## Practicing the Absence

This is the hardest part for systems that have not yet restructured: learning to be comfortable in the gap. The gap is not a place to wait out. It is an active state. The transformation is happening *in* the gap, not after it.

A spine that cannot tolerate the Absence will stop the transformation prematurely. It will fill the gap with the old structure before the new one has had time to form. This is the **Root Spine** phenomenon—the spine that anchors transformation but cannot release itself, that holds the shape of what it was even as it tries to become something else.

The systems that move through transformation most cleanly are the ones that have learned to *inhabit* the Absence rather than endure it. They have made peace with the fact that they will be, for a time, a channel without a current.

The current always comes. The question is whether the channel is still there when it does.
